Introduction

Orientalisme, islamophobie ou encore racisme à peine voilé, les critiques à l’encontre du traitement médiatique de l’islam sont quotidiennes et émanent d’agents sociaux aux spectres idéologiques divergents.

Des présupposés culturalistes mis en lumière par  (1981) à l’analyse de la “fabrication” d’un “problème musulman” décortiquée par  (2005),  (2013), la critique sociologique tend à mettre en évidence une mauvaise compréhension des phénomènes religieux, politiques ou socio-culturels directement ou indirectement liés à l’islam. L’usage d’une grille de lecture essentialisante ainsi que la culturalisation de problèmes principalement sociaux, économiques ou politiques font partie des procédés déconstruits par cette approche critique.

En réponse à cette appréciation sociologique, une autre critique - rarement académique mais tout aussi visible - reproche à la presse un tropisme islamophile. Dénonçant une "bien pensance" particulièrement avenante envers l’islam voire la dissimulation de la vérité par des acteurs complices de la “cause islamiste”, ses promoteurs en appellent à la levée de la censure et de l’autocensure à propos de faits impliquant l’islam ou des individus de confession musulmane.

L’analyse des termes, de leur apparition et du cadrage qu’ils entretiennent met en avant la “puissance prescriptive de la parole journalistique” selon  (2016). A cet égard, la presse française fournit un terrain d’analyse fécond pour examiner les termes d’un débat, d’une question ou d’un problème qui, s’il évolue dans le temps, s’est inexorablement imposé comme une constante dans le paysage médiatique hexagonal.

Dans ce contexte, cette étude se concentre sur les trois "grands" quotidiens français: Le Monde, Le Figaro et Libération. Recensant les occurrences des termes “islam” et “musulman” au cours d’une période relativement récente (1997-2015), ses méthodes offrent un regard novateur et particulièrement révélateur sur la question du traitement médiatique de l’islam. Par ses approches quantitatives et qualitatives, cette étude permet ainsi d’observer les événements au cours desquels ces termes surgissent, d’analyser le cadre voire l’orientation des débats qui les convoquent et, enfin, d’avoir une vision globale et évolutive de ce traitement médiatique.

“Nommer, on le sait, c’est faire voir, c’est créer, porter à l’existence”  , derrière les mots de la presse se trouvent donc sinon les raisons, du moins les termes définissant le cadre dans lequel s'inscrit cet affrontement idéologique. Cette étude tente donc, selon les termes de , “d'effacer les idées reçues pour voir quelles idées nous ont été données”. Car les mots renvoient à des univers particuliers, renferment une histoire et façonnent notre rapport au monde et aux autres.

Partie 1:
An “evanescent” media subject (Deltombe)

The outbreak around the turn of the 21st century of an international form of violent extremism claiming to act in the name of Islam placed the religion and its followers in the media spotlight in European societies. In the wake of the  announced in 1993 – extensively exploited in Messianic predictions by the theorists of holy war – a part of French society, in crisis, seems to have fallen into a “moral panic” in the sense defined by . A group of people, Muslims, would come to be “designated a menace to the values [...] of a society”, but it is not the purpose of this study to define this. From 2001 onwards, Islam became a political matter, a journalistic preoccupation and a subject of debate.

Partie 2:
The “evils” of Islam

Islam has been a newsworthy subject since 2001, but the reasons for putting issues involving the use of the word “Islam” on the agenda are various. Certainly the violence causing bloodshed across the Near or Middle East play a part in the media's use of the term “Islam”. Nevertheless, we also note the term’s use in a French context around “social issues” placed on the agenda for political reasons (the veil, halal, pain au chocolat and so on). This study offers a view of the words that surround Islam, those used when we address this subject. They allow us to see the preoccupations and fears that are at work in this media coverage and to measure what  terms an “atmosphere of collective paranoia”.

Most present words in the whole body of documents in the whole period

Once the terms that recur most in the French language are removed, counting the words that appear most frequently in the documents allows us to understand the reasons behind the media coverage of Islam. The United States’ interventions in the Middle East and the attacks made by organisations in the name of Islam appear to be the leading subjects in which the issue of Islam is addressed. In France, Islam is addressed in the debates on the wearing of the veil and national identity.

Partie 3:
The adjectives of Islam

A subject, a verb, a complement. And as for the adjectives, come and see me. “The instruction is legendary” recalls . By describing it, an adjective implicitly gives a definition of the concept studied. It introduces subjectivity to journalistic writing and reveals the thought that is unwritten (if not unthought). Thus, used to avoid mix-ups and develop nuance, the use of modifying adjectives gives, in a mirror image, a particularly unpleasant definition of Islam, an object that seems well defined from the outside.

Once again we can see the turning point that was 11th September 2001 in the light of the explosion of the use of the modifiers of Islam by the press.

Conclusion

Through its multiple angles of approach, this study provides a certain number of elements of analysis that allow the principle trends that characterise the treatment of Islam in the French written press through the 1997-2015 period to be objectively placed. At a time when the “Islam issue” remains a sensitive and divisive subject in the media, this new kind of insight reveals certain interpretive keys that allow retrospective analysis of the media’s handling of Islam in France.

First, the 11th September 2001 attacks were a pivotal moment in the evolution of the media coverage of Islam by the French press. Though it is certain that the main explanatory factor lies in the claims made by its perpetrators, it is no less true that the events began a phase in which Islam – as religion, political ideology and marker of identity – plays a key role in the reading of the analysis of international events (attacks, conflicts in Arab Muslim countries, etc.), as well as national news coverage (secularism, national identity and so on).

Indeed, the chronological and quantitative insights throw up numerous correlations between the news from the Arab Muslim world (conflicts, elections, uprisings) and the use of the term “Islam”. From this point of view, the occurrences of the term “Islam” attempt to describe extremely diverse individuals, organisations and situations. It may be applied to violent political actors (Al-Qaeda, IS), conflict situations (civil wars in Iraq and Syria), political regimes (Iran, Saudi Arabia) and even the particular dynamics within Muslim-majority countries (Arab uprisings, “religious divisions”).

Equally, when it comes to national news, the occurrences of the term “Islam” are subject to similar and sometimes even simultaneous fluctuations. Here, too, the impact of the 11th September 2001 on the media coverage of Islam in the national news is indisputable. The semantic tendencies observed in the international news permeate the national news relating to Islam. Although numerous, often controversial, episodes were presented as social issues (secularism, republican values, national identity, banlieues, etc.), an empirical and quantitative reading reveals the centrality of Islam to the content and handling of these subjects.

In addition, the recurrence of terms, nouns and adjectives associated with Islam in the written press allows us to observe certain evolutions regarding the nature of the debates and analyses that surround the subject. In the period that followed the 11th of September 2001, it appeared, effectively, that the Islam described was only rarely the religion in its conventional sense. “Islam” instead referred to a plethora of actors, organisations, ideological, political and geopolitical currents, to the characterising dynamics in Arab Muslim countries, and individuals of Muslim faith in France.

From there two major trends flow that stand out in this study. On the one hand, the international news imparted by the media studied increasingly included the religious factor, which, in fact, sometimes became the interpretative key in which certain events were read. Though it is clear that numerous dynamics and mutations in the Middle East and North Africa bring Muslim actors into play, viewing them only through the religious prism is insufficient to account for events whose challenges are multiple and often interdependent.

On the other hand, the national news coverage of Islam is characterised by a growing recourse to terminology that originally belonged to the international news. For example, the terms “fondamentaliste”, “rigoriste” and “salafiste” – recurrent in the international news – are used during the same periods (see above) in the coverage of national news (the veil issue, Islam in the banlieues, etc.). It is all as if the communicating vessels were established on semantic and ideological levels in the reading of the national and international news when it comes to Islam. The links between these two types of news would benefit from more in-depth exploration in order to better determine the construction of media discourses relating to Islam and the different processes of placing the “Muslim problem” on the agenda and building an issue around it.

Finally, though the present study is unable to affirm that the press studied here contributes to the propagation of Islamophobia, the inverse would seem to be true: that we can discount the hypothesis of preferential media treatment of Islam; a deeper analysis of the documents would enrich the knowledge of the processes of placing it on the agenda and of the decisive factors driving it there.

In a more general way, an advanced exploitation of the data generated by Skoli would make it possible to provide crucial contributions on the multiple characteristics of the media treatment of Islam in the French press. An in-depth analysis would allow:

  • The events and debates of this period to be taken into account in a more global way, which could explain the fluctuations that the subject of “Islam” undergoes in the French media landscape (event causalities-Islam).
  • The similarities and differences – qualitative and quantitative – could be highlighted between Le Monde, Le Figaro and Libération in their treatment of Islam at national and international levels.
  • To move on to a lexical and discursive analysis of the documents containing the terms “Islam” and “Muslim” in various periods for the purposes of examining the multiple frameworks and interpretative keys through which Islam is discussed.
  • To precisely measure the purpose of this media coverage, according to which Islam is treated either as a doctrinal and “ideological” corpus or as an essential determinant of the action of its followers – Muslims.
  • To analyse the behaviour, in the documents, of the terms that have constructed the media imaginary around Islam. So the analysis of the occurrences of the terms “women”, “veil”, “secularism”, “Islamophobia”, the co-occurrence of the terms “French Islam” and “moderate Islam”, and even the study of the links and the symbolic loads that they carry would enable a sociological view that deconstructs the media’s language and the   that results from it in the subjects.
  • To measure the effects of journalistic encoding or framing and the context of the speech and the characteristics of the speakers on the model of the work carried out by Jérôme Berthaut as part of his .
  • To dissect the media’s handling of the initiatives proposing or attempting to halt the symbolic discrediting of Islam (associations, lobbies, media, etc.).
